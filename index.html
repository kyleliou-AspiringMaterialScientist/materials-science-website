How Materials Work

The properties of materials begin at the atomic scale. Understanding atoms, bonding, and structures allows engineers to design new materials.

Atomic Structure

Every material begins with atoms. The arrangement and behavior of atoms determine how a material performs.

  • Atomic radius
  • Electron structure
  • Periodic trends
  • Element composition

Chemical Bonding

The forces holding atoms together influence strength, flexibility, conductivity, and stability.

  • Covalent bonds
  • Ionic bonds
  • Metallic bonds
  • Van der Waals forces

Crystal Structures

The arrangement of atoms in solids creates unique material properties.

  • FCC - Face Centered Cubic
  • BCC - Body Centered Cubic
  • HCP - Hexagonal Close Packed
  • Diamond Cubic

Mechanical Properties

How materials respond to forces.

Strength • Hardness • Toughness • Elasticity

Electrical Properties

How materials interact with electricity.

Conductors • Semiconductors • Insulators

Thermal Properties

How materials behave under temperature changes.

Heat Capacity • Conductivity • Expansion

How Materials Are Made

Materials science is not only about discovering materials, but also creating and processing them.

Casting

Liquid metal is poured into molds and cooled to create complex shapes.

  • Automotive parts
  • Metal components
  • Industrial equipment

Forging

Materials are shaped using force to improve strength and durability.

  • Aircraft components
  • Tools
  • High-strength alloys

3D Printing

Additive manufacturing builds objects layer by layer.

  • Medical implants
  • Custom parts
  • Rapid prototyping

Electroplating

A thin layer of metal is deposited onto a surface using electrochemical reactions.

  • Corrosion protection
  • Improved conductivity
  • Surface engineering

Chemical Vapor Deposition

Materials are deposited atom-by-atom from chemical gases.

  • Semiconductor fabrication
  • Graphene production
  • Protective coatings

Heat Treatment

Controlled heating and cooling changes a material's internal structure.

  • Hardening
  • Strength improvement
  • Stress relief

How Scientists Test Materials

Engineers analyze materials to understand their strength, structure, and performance.

Tensile Testing

Measures how materials respond to pulling forces.

Strength • Elasticity • Failure

Hardness Testing

Determines resistance to indentation and wear.

Durability • Surface properties

Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM)

Creates detailed images of material surfaces at microscopic scales.

Microstructure • Defects

X-Ray Diffraction (XRD)

Identifies crystal structures and phases inside materials.

Atoms • Crystals • Phases

Fatigue Testing

Studies how materials behave under repeated loading.

Reliability • Lifespan

Thermal Analysis

Measures how materials change with temperature.

Melting • Expansion • Stability
